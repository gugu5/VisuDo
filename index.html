La personnalité en 5 dimensions

It is now accepted that personality is a main predictor of sporting life course and/or high-level performance. Personality can be defined as “psychological qualities that contribute to an individual’s enduring and distinctive patterns of feeling, thinking and behaving” (Cervone & Pervin, 2016). Literature about the importance of personality regarding talent development and/or high-level performance is abundant, assessing psychological factors such as a person’s confidence, self-esteem, and resilience as capital. Now that psychological factors are accepted as major predictors, different methods of measurements need to be used to see and understand the big picture (Hauw et al., 2021). Many of these tools, such as the PfPI or the TD12, are based on the Five Factor Model of Personality. Many ways of identifying personality and its components have been tried through time. After years of arguing and discussions, a consensus has been found with the five-factor model or so called “big-five” (McCrae & Costa, 2008; McCrae & John, 1992). It is the most widely accepted model of personality trait structure. (Allen et al., 2013; Rolland, 2013). The five-factor model adopts the basic tenets of trait theory (e.g. cross-situational consistency) and contends that the five personality dimensions named extraversion, neuroticism, openness, agreeableness and conscientiousness each encompass a number of more specific traits (termed facets). Emotional stability (or neuroticism) refers to the extent to which a person is calm and poised and includes the facets of sensitiveness, self-confidence, stress vulnerability, and frustration tolerance. Extraversion refers to the extent to which a person is social and talkative and includes the facets of enthusiasm, sociability, energy, and assertiveness. Openness to experience refers to the extent to which a person is imaginative and curious and includes the facets of innovativeness and creativity, intellectual vs. action orientation, self-observation, and oppenness to change. Agreeableness refers to the extent to which a person is gentle and cooperative and includes the facets of competitiveness, orientation towards others, trust, and compliance.
